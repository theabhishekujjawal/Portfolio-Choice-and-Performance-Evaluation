I’m excited to share a new quantitative finance project from my MSc Quantitative Finance work at UCD Smurfit: Portfolio Choice and Performance Evaluation.

In this project, I analysed how mean-variance optimized portfolios perform when built using historical U.S. industry and Fama-French factor data. The work focuses on a practical investment problem: portfolios can look highly efficient in-sample but become fragile out-of-sample because expected returns and covariances are estimated with noise.

I implemented efficient frontiers, Global Minimum Variance and tangency portfolios, Bayes-Stein mean shrinkage, Ledoit-Wolf covariance shrinkage, Capital Market Lines, Fama-French factor models, out-of-sample testing, and mutual fund performance benchmarking. I also compared unconstrained optimization with more realistic approaches such as shrinkage, resampling, and position limits.

The project strengthened my ability to connect portfolio theory with real-world risk management, statistical testing, Python implementation, and professional investment analysis. It was a useful reminder that strong quant work is not just about finding the highest in-sample Sharpe ratio, but about building methods that remain credible when tested on unseen data.
